📡 SIGNAL CONDITIONS

Our RF signal bureau had a lot to report today. Overall conditions were mixed, with signal strengths ranging from a crisp -65 dBm (strong!) to a bone-chilling -121 dBm (yikes).

The average RSSI came in at -107 dBm, skewing weak for most nodes. Nearly 53% of packets logged below -115 dBm, indicating patchy coverage across parts of the network. Think of it like trying to hear a radio in a thunderstorm—some nodes are crystal clear, others just static.

SNR (Signal-to-Noise Ratio) averaged a modest -7.5 dB, with values swinging between a noisy -21.5 dB and a respectable +8.0 dB. Negative SNR means we’re fighting interference—common in heavily wooded or urban environments.

When it comes to hop counts, 3-hop and 4-hop packets dominated, each accounting for about 22% of traffic. That’s a good sign—the mesh is doing its job, relaying messages across long distances. Only 47 packets came in direct (zero-hop), underscoring how much of our network depends on intermediate nodes.

On the bright side, HOONET-1 delivered strong signals at -69 dBm, and HOONET-2 held steady at -80 dBm—both textbook examples of solid mesh propagation.

But don’t forget: our base station hiccup meant we missed 7 hours of signal data, so take these readings as a partial picture of an active, noisy, and occasionally brilliant mesh.
